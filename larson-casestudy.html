Case Study: Optimizing Gas Station Repairs for Oscar Larson

Introduction

YOscar Larson is a company specializing in gas station maintenance and repair services. One of their biggest operational challenges was inefficiency in dispatching repair trucks. When a gas station required repairs, a technician would visit the site to diagnose the issue, only to realize that the necessary tools or parts were missing. This resulted in multiple trips, increasing costs and reducing service efficiency.

To solve this problem, we developed a data-driven predictive system that leveraged Oscar Larson’s legacy database of past repairs. Our solution recommended the right parts and tools for each service request, reducing unnecessary trips and improving first-time fix rates.

The Challenge: Noisy Legacy Data & Inefficient Dispatches

Oscar Larson had a vast but unstructured historical database of past service reports from gas stations. However, the data was:

  • Noisy and Incomplete: Many records had missing or inconsistent part descriptions.
  • Unstructured: Data entries lacked standardization, with technicians using different terminologies for the same parts.
  • Not Optimized for Decision-Making: There was no system in place to extract actionable insights from past repairs.
  • High Burn, Low Output: Despite significant investments, the agency's deliverables were slow, unoptimized, and required constant rework.

The goal was to clean and structure this data, then build an inference system that could predict the required tools and parts based on past repair histories for similar issues.

Data Cleaning & Standardization

Before we could use the legacy data effectively, we performed extensive preprocessing:

  • Data Normalization: Standardized part names using NLP-based fuzzy matching to merge duplicates (e.g., “Fuel Pump Assy” vs. “Fuel Pump Assembly”).
  • Handling Missing Data: Implemented an imputation strategy where missing part data was filled based on similar service requests.
  • Standardizing Technician Notes: Used text classification models to categorize technician notes into predefined issue categories.
  • Outlier Detection: Identified and removed erroneous entries using anomaly detection techniques.

Predictive Inference Algorithm

Once the data was structured, we built an inference system that suggested parts and tools based on historical patterns. The core algorithm followed these steps:

  1. Feature Engineering: Extracted key attributes from historical repair logs, including:
    • Gas station type (e.g., highway vs. urban)
    • Past reported issues
    • Repair frequency
    • Technician-reported symptoms
  2. Similarity Matching: Implemented a k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) model to find past repairs similar to the new service request.
  3. Probabilistic Recommendations: Used Bayesian inference to predict the most likely required parts based on past repair distributions.
  4. Confidence Scoring: Assigned confidence levels to each part recommendation, helping technicians prioritize what to carry.
  5. Continuous Learning: The system improved over time by incorporating feedback from technician confirmations on whether the suggested parts were correct.

Results & Impact

After deploying the solution, Oscar Larson saw significant improvements:

  • 30% Reduction in Unnecessary Trips: Technicians arrived equipped with the correct tools more often.
  • 20% Faster Repair Times: Less downtime at gas stations due to fewer back-and-forth trips.
  • Increased Technician Efficiency: Reduced frustration and improved job satisfaction among technicians.
  • Better Data Utilization: Legacy repair data was transformed from a liability into a valuable decision-making tool.

Conclusion

By leveraging historical repair data and implementing predictive inference models, we transformed how Oscar Larson managed service dispatches. The solution not only reduced operational costs but also improved service efficiency and customer satisfaction. This case study highlights the power of data cleaning, standardization, and intelligent inference in solving real-world logistics and field service challenges.
